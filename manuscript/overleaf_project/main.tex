\begin{abstract}
Reliable rock-fragmentation monitoring is useful for blast assessment and mine-to-mill control, but real rockpile point clouds rarely provide point-level fragment labels or independent particle-size-distribution (PSD) references. This paper presents a synthetic exterior point-cloud benchmark designed as a pre-field testbed for visible-surface PSD proxy estimation. The workflow starts from a library of synthetic rock-like fragment meshes with known volume and identity. These fragments are placed into labelled rockpile scenes, converted to exterior-only point-cloud scans, and used to train or calibrate surface-clustering methods. The main learning target is not a scene-specific fragment-ID class; instead, a DGCNN/EdgeConv model predicts whether neighbouring exterior points belong to the same fragment. Connected components of high-affinity edges become visible-surface clusters, and those clusters are converted into PSD and P80 proxy estimates. The latest benchmark run uses 100 no-boundary synthetic exterior scenes with 150 requested fragments per scene and a 60/20/20 train/validation/test split. EdgeConv reached validation average precision of 0.9313 after 24 epochs, but its selected post-split connected-component setting produced held-out mean absolute P80 error of 19.04\% with mean noise fraction of 0.603. Re-evaluating other methods on the same 150-fragment split showed that a calibrated hand-crafted graph-threshold rule achieved lower P80 error, 14.37\%, but with high noise fraction and weak ARI; MLP affinity, DBSCAN, and normal/curvature region growing exposed different trade-offs between P80 error and instance quality. The results show that same-fragment affinity can be learned, but robust graph partitioning, threshold calibration, and post-processing remain the main obstacles before field deployment.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} rock fragmentation; synthetic rockpile; exterior point cloud; visible-surface PSD proxy; EdgeConv; DGCNN; particle size distribution; P80; photogrammetry; mining

\section{Introduction}
Post-blast fragmentation affects loading, hauling, crushing, and comminution. Excessive fines or oversize material can lower equipment productivity and increase energy demand, so fragmentation monitoring is relevant to mine-to-mill decision support \cite{cunningham1983,ouchterlony2005}. Operational systems have traditionally relied on two-dimensional images, including commercial and research workflows for visible fragmentation assessment \cite{palangio1995,siddiqui2009}. Those systems are practical, but a projected image must infer scale, overlap, occlusion, and hidden fragment boundaries from a single visible surface.

Three-dimensional photogrammetry and lidar point clouds provide richer surface geometry. They preserve relative position, local slope, and surface continuity, and are therefore attractive for muckpile analysis \cite{westoby2012,anderson2019,engin2020,onederra2015}. Nevertheless, a rockpile point cloud still observes only the exterior surface. Buried contacts and the back sides of fragments are unavailable. Consequently, a PSD estimated from a surface point cloud should be interpreted as a visible-surface proxy unless it is calibrated against independent field measurements.

The distinction between a measured PSD and a visible-surface PSD proxy is central to this paper. A full synthetic pile contains all fragment surfaces and all labels, but a camera or scanner does not. If a learning benchmark uses hidden contact surfaces, the target becomes easier than the field problem and may overstate model performance. The benchmark reported here therefore asks a narrower and more reproducible question: from labelled synthetic rock fragments arranged into rockpiles, how much useful P80 proxy information can be recovered when the learning model sees only exterior points?

The contributions are fourfold. First, the paper describes the complete benchmark narrative from fragment generation through rockpile construction and exterior-only scan extraction. Second, it explains why edge-affinity learning is better posed than direct fragment-ID classification for multi-scene synthetic rockpiles. Third, it motivates the use of DGCNN/EdgeConv over purely geometric clustering or global point classifiers for this task. Fourth, it reports the latest 100-scene no-boundary synthetic run and interprets the result as a surface-cluster PSD proxy rather than solved fragment instance segmentation.

\section{Related Work and Benchmark Positioning}
Fragmentation measurement sits between blasting, materials handling, crushing, and sustainability. Blast outcomes influence downstream energy use, productivity, rehandling, and oversize management, so fragmentation monitoring is part of a broader mine-to-mill control problem rather than a purely visual segmentation problem \cite{laurence2011,cunningham1983,ouchterlony2005}. Image-based systems such as WipFrag and related digital image-processing approaches have been widely used because cameras are cheap and operationally convenient \cite{palangio1995,siddiqui2009}. Their limitation is not usefulness; it is the unavoidable ambiguity of inferring three-dimensional particle size from a two-dimensional visible surface.

Three-dimensional reconstruction methods reduce some of that ambiguity. Structure-from-motion, multi-view stereo, and lidar workflows have become common in geoscience because they recover scale, surface orientation, and spatial context \cite{westoby2012,eltner2016,anderson2019}. In rockpile applications, lidar-based measurement has demonstrated the potential of 3D data for blasted-rock size estimation \cite{engin2020,onederra2015}. Yet these data sources still observe only exposed surfaces. A surface scan cannot see buried material, so a fair benchmark must separate visible-surface proxy recovery from full-volume PSD measurement.

Point-cloud learning offers useful tools for this exterior-surface problem, but the target must be chosen carefully. PointNet and PointNet++ established strong point-set learning baselines, while DGCNN introduced EdgeConv for local graph-based feature learning \cite{qi2017pointnet,qi2017pointnetpp,wang2019}. Classical clustering methods such as DBSCAN remain important because they provide interpretable non-learning baselines \cite{ester1996}. However, cluster quality and PSD quality are not identical. Partition metrics such as adjusted Rand index and normalized mutual information are useful diagnostics \cite{hubert1985,vinh2010}, but a P80 proxy can be sensitive to a small number of large over-merged or over-split components. The benchmark therefore reports both edge-learning behaviour and PSD proxy behaviour.

\section{Materials and Methods}
\subsection{Synthetic Fragment Mesh Library}
The benchmark begins before pile construction. A library of rock-like triangular fragment meshes is generated with the Synthetic\_Rockpile workflow. Each mesh has a persistent fragment identifier, volume, equivalent spherical diameter, and mesh path. This is important because the learning labels do not originate from a manual drawing on a point cloud; they originate from the synthetic fragment identity before the pile exists.

The fragments are irregular convex to weakly angular meshes rather than spheres. Their role is not to prove that the synthetic fracture process perfectly reproduces blasted rock. Their role is to provide a controlled, labelled population in which each fragment can be selected, placed, sampled, hidden, exposed, and evaluated without losing its identity. In real field scans, fragment labels and full-volume PSD references are difficult or impossible to obtain at point level. Here, those references are known by construction.

For each scene, 150 fragments are sampled without replacement from the fragment catalogue. Larger fragments are not assigned a special semantic class; they simply carry larger volumes and equivalent diameters. The reference PSD for a scene is computed from these known fragment volumes using equivalent spherical diameter. This reference is independent of the model prediction, so the downstream P80 comparison tests the complete exterior-clustering pipeline rather than rewarding a method for reproducing its own clusters.

\subsection{Rockpile Construction Backend}
The generated fragments are then arranged into rockpile scenes. Several options were tested during development, including the original Synthetic\_Rockpile cone/drop heuristic, sequential-drop soft-sphere DEM, Project Chrono convex-hull contact, and clump contact. The fully dynamic sequential-drop variants were visually attractive but were too expensive or numerically sensitive for producing 100 repeatable no-boundary scenes. In particular, boundary-free sequential release tended to roll fragments away from the pile or produce contact-instability outliers unless the release interval, damping, and contact representation were tuned very conservatively.

The reported dataset therefore uses the physics-informed realistic-rockpile generator in a no-boundary envelope-relax configuration with axis-clump contact. The method is best understood as a soft-sphere DEM surrogate for creating stable labelled piles, not as a calibrated FDEM blast simulation. Initial fragment centres are sampled inside a compact pile envelope. Gravity, damping, ground contact, and fragment-fragment contact are then integrated for a fixed number of steps. The visual geometry remains the original fragment mesh; the contact representation is an axis-aligned clump approximation used for stable, faster collision response.

\subsection{Material and Contact Interpretation}
The density value, 2650 kg m$^{-3}$, corresponds to a generic hard-rock density and is used to convert known mesh volumes into masses for the DEM surrogate. The contact parameters are numerical DEM parameters rather than laboratory-calibrated elastic moduli. In other words, the reported generator does not claim to reproduce grain-scale fracture, asperity-scale contact, or complete blast muckpile mechanics. The parameters were selected to produce stable piles with plausible height, base radius, and visible-fragment exposure while retaining exact fragment labels.

This distinction matters for interpretation. A high-fidelity field simulator would require calibrated rock stiffness, fracture, shape distribution, bench geometry, blast throw, and post-blast interaction. The present generator is narrower: it provides labelled, repeatable exterior point clouds for training and testing segmentation-to-PSD algorithms. It is therefore suitable as a pre-field benchmark, while field deployment still requires independent validation against real muckpile scans and PSD references.

\subsection{Mesh Placement and Exterior Point-Cloud Sampling}
After DEM relaxation, the original fragment meshes are placed at their final centres and orientations. Surface points are then sampled from the placed meshes in proportion to fragment volume, with a minimum point allocation for small fragments. The full sampled pile still contains hidden surfaces: underside points, buried contact surfaces, and internal surfaces that would not be visible to a camera or lidar scanner. These points are useful for label transfer but are not used directly for training.

\subsection{Exterior-Only Scan Construction}
The exterior scan construction is deliberately stricter than simple random surface sampling. A real photogrammetric or lidar scan observes only exposed surfaces from finite viewpoints, so the synthetic benchmark removes hidden and contact-like samples before graph construction. First, five default viewpoints are placed around the pile: four side viewpoints and one overhead viewpoint. For each viewpoint, points are binned in azimuth-elevation angle, and only the nearest point in each angular bin is retained. The angular bin width is 0.24 degrees in the reported dataset.

Second, the union of viewpoint-visible points is filtered by a plan-view height envelope. Points are binned in the $x$-$y$ plane using a 0.035 m grid. Within each grid cell, the local maximum elevation is estimated, and only points within 0.030 m of that upper envelope are retained. This second pass is important because line-of-sight filtering on a point-sampled synthetic pile can still keep contact or interior samples through small gaps. The height-envelope filter changes the problem from full-pile surface grouping to upper-exterior surface grouping, which is closer to what an operational scan would provide.

After exterior filtering, a small coordinate perturbation with standard deviation 0.0015 m is added, and a random density keep fraction between 0.78 and 0.92 is applied to mimic non-uniform point-cloud density. Normals and curvature are estimated from the exterior points using 30 nearest neighbours. A $k=12$ nearest-neighbour graph is then built on the exterior point coordinates. Edge labels are exact: an edge is positive if both endpoint points retain the same original fragment identity and negative otherwise.

\section{Why Edge Affinity and Why DGCNN/EdgeConv?}
Direct point classification into fragment identifiers is poorly posed across scenes. Fragment 17 in one pile has no semantic relation to fragment 17 in another pile. A transferable model should therefore learn a relation, not a fixed label vocabulary. The benchmark uses edge affinity: for each edge in a local neighbourhood graph, the model predicts whether the two endpoint points belong to the same underlying fragment.

This edge-affinity formulation also matches the physical structure of the problem. Fragment boundaries are local. A point belongs with its neighbours if their positions, normals, curvature, and edge geometry are consistent with the same visible rock surface. Conversely, neighbouring points on different fragments may be spatially close but separated by a change in normal direction, height jump, curvature, or local surface continuity.

DGCNN/EdgeConv was selected because it learns local geometric relationships on point-cloud graphs \cite{wang2019}. Compared with a global PointNet-style descriptor \cite{qi2017pointnet}, EdgeConv better preserves local boundary information. Compared with PointNet++ \cite{qi2017pointnetpp}, the implementation is compact and directly suited to edge-pair supervision. Compared with a simple MLP edge classifier, EdgeConv embeds each point using graph neighbourhood context before scoring an edge, so the edge decision is not based only on hand-designed pair features. Compared with DBSCAN or normal/curvature region growing \cite{ester1996}, it can learn how multiple cues trade off rather than relying on a fixed radius, angle, or smoothness threshold.

These advantages do not make DGCNN a magic fragment segmenter. They make it a reasonable controlled baseline for the transferable part of the problem: learning same-fragment affinity from exterior geometry when labels are available synthetically.

\section{Training Protocol}
Point features include normalized coordinates, normals, and curvature. Edge features encode local geometric differences and surface continuity cues. During training, edges are sampled in balanced positive/negative batches because same-fragment and different-fragment edges are naturally imbalanced in a local graph. Training is performed scene by scene to avoid leakage between neighbouring points from the same pile across train and validation splits.

The latest run trained the EdgeConv model for 24 epochs. The maximum number of sampled training edges per scene was 22,000, the maximum number of validation edges per scene was 36,000, and validation metrics were evaluated on 12 validation scenes per epoch for efficiency. Photogrammetry-realism augmentation was retained during training with strength 0.75. This augmentation perturbs point coordinates, normals, curvature, and edge geometry while preserving labels, approximating common reconstruction imperfections without changing the held-out synthetic ground truth.

At inference, edge probabilities are thresholded and connected components become predicted visible-surface clusters. Four variants are evaluated: raw connected components, affinity-based absorption of unlabelled points, height-marker splitting of oversized clusters, and the combined absorb-plus-post-split variant. Threshold selection is performed only on the validation split. Test scenes are held out until the selected variant and threshold are frozen.

\section{PSD Proxy Evaluation}
Predicted components are converted into visible-surface PSD proxies. For each predicted component, a surface-cluster diameter proxy is estimated from the spatial span of its points. Proxy volumes are then accumulated into a volume-weighted passing curve, from which P80 is interpolated. This is intentionally called a proxy because exterior points are not closed fragments and do not observe buried volume.

The evaluation reports both edge-learning and downstream PSD behaviour. Average precision and ROC-AUC measure how well the model ranks same-fragment edges. P80 error measures whether the resulting connected components preserve enough size information for a volume-weighted surface proxy. Noise fraction, normalized mutual information (NMI), and adjusted Rand index (ARI) diagnose whether predicted components resemble fragment instances. These metrics can disagree: a threshold can improve P80 while still producing weak instance segmentation, or improve cluster purity while fragmenting large rocks and hurting P80.

\section{Results}
The EdgeConv model learned edge affinity clearly. Training loss decreased from 0.5722 at epoch 1 to 0.3479 at epoch 24. Validation average precision increased from 0.8569 to 0.9313, and validation ROC-AUC reached 0.9179.

The photogrammetry-realism augmentation used in the latest run was retained from the earlier JSM-style benchmark, where an otherwise matched EdgeConv run without augmentation gave higher P80 error and weaker clustering diagnostics. For the present 150-fragment dataset, the no-augmentation ablation was not repeated; instead, all learned baselines reported below use the same exterior scan target and the same 60/20/20 scene split.

The MLP edge-affinity baseline was retrained on the 150-fragment dataset for the same 24 epochs used by EdgeConv. It uses the same saved edge geometry features but does not use an EdgeConv graph encoder. Its training loss decreased from 0.5524 at epoch 1 to 0.5363 at epoch 24, and validation balanced accuracy at a fixed 0.5 edge threshold stayed near 0.736. This weaker learning curve is consistent with the idea that graph-context encoding matters for local boundary decisions, even when downstream P80 can be partially recovered by threshold calibration.

The downstream connected-component calibration remained difficult. The validation sweep selected the EdgeConv post-split variant at threshold 0.997 for the transfer setting used in the previous EdgeConv-only evaluation. On the 20 held-out test scenes, this setting produced a mean absolute P80 error of 19.04\%, a median absolute P80 error of 18.36\%, and a mean noise fraction of 0.603. Raw EdgeConv components gave 20.82\% mean absolute P80 error. Absorption reduced the noise fraction to approximately 0.506 but worsened P80 error in this run.

\section{Discussion}
The result should be read carefully because the latest 150-fragment comparison separates PSD proxy accuracy from fragment instance quality. The hand-crafted graph-threshold rule achieved the lowest held-out mean absolute P80 error, 14.37\%, but it also produced a high noise fraction of 0.694 and very low ARI. It is therefore a useful P80-oriented engineering rule, not evidence of clean fragment segmentation. Conversely, region growing produced the strongest NMI and ARI among the non-neural baselines but over-merged visible surfaces so severely that P80 error exceeded 100\%. This confirms that instance-style metrics and PSD/P80 metrics can disagree.

EdgeConv remains valuable, but its role is more specific than a simple winner-takes-all result. The model learned same-fragment edge affinity clearly, reaching validation AP of 0.9313, and it provides a transferable graph-learning baseline for arbitrary fragment IDs. However, its post-split connected-component calibration produced 19.04\% held-out P80 error, slightly worse than the calibrated graph-threshold and MLP baselines in this run. The main weakness is therefore not only edge learning; it is the conversion from edge probabilities to PSD-relevant components.

The MLP affinity result supports the need for graph context. It was trained for the same 24 epochs on the same latest split but showed a weak validation learning curve and very high test noise fraction. DBSCAN was competitive in P80 and had lower noise than EdgeConv, but its behaviour is controlled by a fixed density radius and is expected to be sensitive to point density and scale. These comparisons justify reporting EdgeConv alongside simpler baselines rather than presenting it as an already solved operational model.

The next technical step should focus on graph partitioning and calibration. Candidate improvements include adaptive local thresholds, learned graph partitioning, morphology-constrained merge/split rules, PSD-aware validation objectives, and multi-objective selection that reports both P80 error and instance diagnostics. Simply adding more synthetic scenes or more epochs is unlikely to solve the threshold-noise trade-off by itself.

\section{Limitations and Field Validation}
This study reports a synthetic exterior-surface benchmark. It does not report field P80 accuracy. Real muckpile deployment requires independent references such as sieve or belt sampling, calibrated image-analysis outputs, carefully scaled manual annotation, or expert delineation on high-resolution orthomosaics. A field study should freeze the synthetic-trained model and threshold-selection rule before evaluating real scans.

The synthetic piles also remain simplified. The production preset was selected because it is stable and practical for 100 scenes, not because it fully reproduces blast mechanics. More realistic DEM/FDEM or Chrono-based generation may improve morphology, but only if contact stability, compute cost, and exterior sampling are controlled. The present benchmark is therefore a pre-field testbed: it is useful for developing and comparing algorithms before expensive field validation.

\section{Reproducibility}
The accompanying repository includes source code, manuscript source, figure assets, scene index, training history, validation threshold summaries, and held-out test summaries. The full `.npz` scene files and trained checkpoint are treated as generated artifacts because they can be regenerated from the provided scripts. The intended production command regenerates 100 no-boundary exterior scenes with 150 requested fragments, 800 DEM-relaxation steps, 30,000 requested surface samples per scene, and four CPU workers.

\section{Conclusion}
The manuscript reframes the project as a complete synthetic exterior rockpile benchmark rather than a short model report. Synthetic fragments are generated with known identity and volume, arranged into labelled rockpiles, converted to exterior-only point clouds, and used to train or calibrate several surface-clustering methods. The latest 100-scene, 150-fragment comparison shows that P80 proxy recovery and clean fragment instance segmentation are different objectives. A calibrated graph-threshold rule achieved the lowest held-out mean absolute P80 error, 14.37\%, while EdgeConv learned edge affinity well but reached 19.04\% P80 error after connected-component post-processing. The next step is therefore not simply more data or longer training; it is better graph partitioning, post-processing, and field validation for PSD-relevant exterior surface clusters.
